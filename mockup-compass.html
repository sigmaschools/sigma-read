Why Do Octopuses Have Blue Blood?

Science · For You · 3 min read

Deep beneath the ocean's surface, octopuses carry a remarkable secret in their veins. Unlike humans, whose blood runs red, octopus blood is bright blue.

This unusual color comes from a molecule called hemocyanin. While human blood uses iron to carry oxygen (which makes it red), octopus blood uses copper instead. When copper binds to oxygen, it turns blue.

But hemocyanin isn't just about color — it's a survival tool. Copper-based blood is much better at carrying oxygen in cold, low-oxygen environments like the deep sea. This is why octopuses can thrive in waters where many other creatures cannot.
